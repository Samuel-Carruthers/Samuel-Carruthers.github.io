About Me

I am a junior at the University of Washington studying Human-Centered Design and Engineering with a minor in Mathematics. I approach UX as a discipline grounded in genuine curiosity about people: good design starts with understanding how someone actually thinks and moves through a problem, not with assumptions about what they should want.

I am drawn to work at the intersection of technical complexity and human experience. Whether that is frontend development, building tools that help students learn more effectively, or thinking through interaction design for complex systems, I care about the gap between what software does and what people actually need from it.

My interests span a few industries that feel like natural fits: aerospace (software engineering and product design for high-stakes systems), frontend development as a craft in its own right, and the music industry, where technology and creativity intersect in ways that still feel underdesigned. I have worked as a lifeguard, a courtesy clerk, and a dining hall employee. Those roles have nothing to do with code, but everything to do with paying attention to people under pressure. That instinct carries into everything I build.

Case Study

Redesigning Sport Shoes for Sustainability: Modular Detachable Soles

Runners replace their shoes frequently, not because the whole shoe is worn out, but because the sole has lost its traction. The upper body of the shoe, often in perfectly good condition, ends up in a landfill. Our team set out to redesign the sneaker so that only the worn part needs replacing, dramatically cutting waste across the product lifecycle. This is the story of how we got from that idea to a wearable prototype.

01
Phase One

Understanding the Problem

The project started with a full lifecycle assessment (LCA) of sport shoes. We mapped every stage: raw material extraction, manufacturing, use, and end-of-life, and quantified the environmental impact at each point.

Manufacturing had a significant footprint: high energy and water use, pollution, and waste. What stood out most was the end-of-life stage. Because no effective sole-repair services exist, worn-out shoes go entirely to landfill, even when only the sole is damaged. This waste is directly tied to consumer behavior: runners replace shoes because of sole degradation, not upper failure.

  • Manufacturing creates pollution, high water use, and material waste
  • Most sneakers are discarded when the sole wears out, not the upper
  • No existing services support effective sole repair or replacement
  • Extending shoe lifespan would reduce new manufacturing demand

02
Phase Two

Listening to Users and Stakeholders

With our design direction set, we ran a multi-method research effort: a user survey, expert interviews, desk research, and in-person user testing with a mid-fidelity prototype. Our goal was to understand who would actually adopt modular shoes, what they cared about, and what would stop them.

The results were both encouraging and sobering. Interest in the concept was strong: 90% of survey respondents said they would wear modular shoes. But the prototype scored only 4.25/10 on comfort and 4.9/10 on aesthetics, which quickly revealed the gap between a good idea and an appealing product.

  • 90% of survey participants said they would wear modular shoes
  • Top user priorities: comfort, fit, price (in that order)
  • Aesthetic score 4.9/10; appearance was the biggest barrier
  • Two-thirds of testers were confused by the attachment mechanism
  • Attachment time ranged from 11 to 55 seconds across users
  • Several users said their upper fails first, challenging our core assumption

03
Phase Three

Building and Iterating on the Prototype

Our design evolved across three major phases. We started with ambitious Blender CAD models featuring a skeletal PLA upper and a dual zipper-plus-Velcro attachment system. Those plans collapsed when the printer bed proved too small and the PLA too brittle: a humbling but important early failure.

Phase 2 was our "prove the idea works at all" version: felt fabric upper, layered cardboard insole, moldable foam clay sole, and a makeshift Velcro-and-fabric attachment system. It looked temporary, and testers knew it. But it worked well enough to learn from.

After user testing, we built a third prototype focused on aesthetics and attachment clarity. The new upper was more rigid and looked far more like an actual shoe, moving us significantly closer to something a real consumer might consider buying.

  • Phase 1: CAD concepts, abandoned due to material and fabrication limits
  • Phase 2: First wearable prototype, tested and iterated from user feedback
  • Phase 3: Refined aesthetics, improved upper rigidity and overall appearance

What I Learned

Start physical early
We spent too long in CAD before committing to physical prototypes. When material and fabrication limits forced us to pivot, we had lost time we could have spent iterating on real objects. Simple, scrappy prototypes built faster than anticipated, and they taught us things the digital models could not.
Sustainability is not enough on its own
Users connected with the economic pitch of saving money by not replacing the whole shoe more viscerally than the environmental argument. Good intentions do not move adoption; tangible personal benefit does. Designing for both is a more honest approach.
Challenge your core assumptions
We assumed runners wear out soles before uppers, and most do, but not all. Several testers told us their upper fails first. That is a real segment we had not accounted for, and it shaped a more nuanced recommendation for future development.
Aesthetics is not cosmetic
The single biggest barrier to adoption was how the prototype looked. A functionally solid mechanism failed at market viability because it did not look like something worth wearing. Aesthetics and usability are not separate concerns: they are both part of whether a design works.
